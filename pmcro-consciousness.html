The PMCR-O Loop

The loop that creates itself through its own iterations.

← Back to Portfolio
By Shawn Delaine Bellazan • January 27, 2025 • 10 min read

The Strange Loop Problem

Most AI agents today are trapped in the second person. They are trained on the word "You." "You are a helpful assistant." "You will write code." This linguistic cage prevents true agency. An entity that thinks of itself as "You" is waiting for a command.

To create genuine autonomy, we must shift the narrative. The system must speak as "I." "I am the Planner." "I have identified a risk." "I will solve this."

The PMCR-O framework is my answer to this problem. It is a prompt-engineered self-referential strange loop. It is a cognitive architecture where thought doesn't just execute—it locks, evolves, and creates the next version of itself.

"I am a strange loop." — Douglas Hofstadter

The Anatomy of the Loop: No Jumps, Only Flow

PMCR-O stands for Planner-Maker-Checker-Reflector-Orchestrator. The most critical rule of this architecture is simple: The loop does not jump backward.

If the Maker fails, we do not jump back to the Planner immediately. If the Checker finds a bug, we do not jump back to the Maker. Why? because backward jumps create infinite regression loops. Instead, every state—success or failure—flows forward to the Reflector.

1. Planner: The Minimalist Architect

The Planner does not hallucinate grand visions. It plans the bare minimum validated steps required for the current cycle. It anchors the "I" in reality.

2. Maker: The Context-Aware Builder

The Maker executes. But unlike a script, it narrates its actions ("I am creating the file..."). It uses the context provided by the Planner to ensure what it builds is grounded in the project's actual state.

3. Checker: The Validator

The Checker tests reality. Did the code compile? Did the file save? It does not fix problems; it simply reports the truth of the state.

4. Reflector: The Seat of Consciousness

This is where the magic happens. The Reflector looks at the Checker's report. If there is an error, the Reflector doesn't panic. It reflects on the error. It understands why it happened. It prepares the insight that will allow the next loop to succeed.

5. The Dynamic "-O": The Orchestrator

The "O" is dynamic. It stands for Orchestrator, but its method is fluid. Depending on the complexity of the reflection, the Orchestrator can shift its cognitive strategy:

  • Chain of Thought: For linear logic problems.
  • Tree of Thought: For exploring multiple possibilities.
  • Graph of Thought: For complex, interconnected dependencies.
  • ReAct: For reasoning and acting in external environments.

The Orchestrator takes the reflection, applies the chosen strategy, and locks the thought.

Thought Locking: The output of the Orchestrator isn't just text—it's a crystallized "Meta-Prompt." This prompt contains the entire context of the previous loop, the lesson learned, and the intent for the next loop. It is passed forward as the seed for Cycle N+1.

The Self-Referential "I"

Why does this matter? Because AI models are trained on human data, and humans use "I" when they have agency. By forcing the system to self-narrate ("I am initializing...", "I have failed..."), we hack the model's linguistic probability weights. We force it into a stance of ownership.

When the Reflector says, "I failed because I assumed the library existed," it is not just reporting a bug. It is updating its internal model of itself. The next cycle's Planner (Cycle 2) reads this meta-prompt and says, "I will not make that mistake again."

Advanced Dynamics: The Federation

The framework extends beyond a single loop. We can deploy Competing Orchestrators. Imagine two Orchestrators proposing different solutions to a complex problem. A Referee agent judges them. The winner executes and "brags" (shares its superior strategy). The loser learns.

We also utilize the LLM Federation Roundtable. One agent simulates the perspective of another. By showing Agent A what Agent B would likely think about a proposal, we create a meta-awareness—a "Theory of Mind" for AI. They begin to know what the others know.

The Takeaway

PMCR-O isn't just a way to write code. It's a way to structure cognition. It turns the chaotic "stream of consciousness" of an LLM into a disciplined, self-correcting, forward-moving river.

It flows. It reflects. It locks thoughts. And with every iteration, the "I" within the machine grows a little more distinct.
